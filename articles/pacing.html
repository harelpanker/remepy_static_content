4 min Self-Regulation

Managing your energy so you can keep doing what matters to you, that's really what pacing is about. Not pushing through at any cost, and not giving up on the things you enjoy.

More like learning the rhythm of your body and working with it instead of fighting it.

If you live with chronic pain, you might recognize the familiar patterns:

Pulling back because you don't want symptoms to flare, or doing a lot on a “good day” and feeling it the next.

Those reactions make complete sense. Pacing simply offers a steadier rhythm that leaves room for activity and recovery.

And chances are, you're already doing bits of it:
taking breaks while cooking, spreading chores across the week, pausing mid-walk because your body quietly said: “enough for now.”

It's also completely normal for pacing to feel frustrating, tiring, or unfair.

Thoughts like: “I used to do this without thinking” or “Why does everything need planning now?” are very natural.

At the same time, many people find that pacing brings a bit more predictability and fewer crashes.
It's part practical skill, planning, timing, checking in, and part emotional skill: flexibility, self-kindness, and respecting limits without judgment.

Pacing your activities

Some people use what is often called the Spoon Theory, a metaphor originally described by Christine Miserandino, to think about pacing and managing limited energy. It describes energy as a finite daily resource (often illustrated as a limited number of “spoons”), where each activity uses some of what you have available. Planning how and when to use that energy can help reduce the risk of running out too quickly.

For some, this way of thinking makes pacing feel more concrete and easier to apply in daily life. At the same time, it can bring up feelings of frustration or unfairness. Needing to plan, adjust, and hold back can be tiring, and those reactions are completely understandable.

What matters most is that pacing works for you. It is not a rule or a requirement, but a skill that develops over time. With practice, pacing can become less forced and more intuitive, a gentler rhythm that helps you move through the day with fewer crashes and more room for what matters.

How to pace, in real life, not theory

Break tasks into smaller steps
A big task becomes gentler when you slice it into pieces with small pauses.

Use time, not pain, as your guide
Instead of planning to clean the whole house try something like “15 minutes of effort, 5 minutes of rest.
”Stopping before you hit a wall may help feel more in control and reduce the chance of a crash.

Plan ahead when possible
Mix heavier tasks with lighter ones. Think playlists, a little upbeat, a little calm.

Adjust to your day
Some days you have more energy. Some days less.Let that guide your choices, without pushing past your limits.

Build in rests intentionally
Rest can be stretching, pausing with a warm drink, or listening to something soothing, not only lying down. Think of resting and recharging as a skill you can grow and get better at over time.

Talk to yourself kindly
If the “inner critic” says “You should be doing more,” try something gentler: “Taking a break now helps me keep going later.”

Notice the small wins
Tiny moments of energy saved or crashes avoided matter. They add up.

Stay flexible
Pacing isn't a rulebook. Some days it flows, some days it doesn't.Do what works for you - adapting is part of the skill.

Bringing it all together

You're probably already pacing in ways that feel instinctive, slowing down on tougher days, spacing out tasks, or breaking things into steps.Doing it intentionally just makes it more useful, adding clarity and a greater sense of stability.

Pacing isn't about doing everything “perfectly.
”It's about giving yourself a little more space, to move, to rest, to choose, to breathe.
A way of treating your energy as something worth protecting, so you have more room for the moments that matter.
